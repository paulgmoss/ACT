The Alignment Construction Tool

How do you know if your outcomes, assignments, and rubrics are truly aligned? This tool visualises the connections between your Course Learning Outcomes and assignments. Start from whichever component feels most natural and see how changes affect your entire course design.

|

Why Use This Tool?

Course design involves many interrelated decisions: how much weight each learning outcome carries, how assignments are structured, and how rubric criteria map back to outcomes. In practice, these decisions are often made separately, and small misalignments can compound across a course without being visible.

This tool makes those relationships explicit. By entering your estimates for CLO weightings and assignment mappings (or vice versa), you can see immediately whether the structure of your course reflects your intentions. Where it does not, the tool highlights the discrepancy so you can make informed adjustments before finalising your course design.

A Note on Terminology

This tool uses the term CLO (Course Learning Outcome). At your institution these may be called ILOs (Intended Learning Outcomes), ULOs (Unit Learning Outcomes), SLOs (Subject Learning Outcomes), or similar. They all refer to the learning outcomes at the subject or course level: the individual unit of study that sits within a degree programme.

This tool also uses the term assignment interchangeably with assessment. Both refer to the tasks students complete that are used to evaluate their learning. The assignments referred to in this tool are summative assignments: those that contribute to the overall course grade.

⇄ How The Tool Works: Two Pathways What you provide, what the tool calculates, and how to iterate

The tool offers two streams depending on where you want to start. Note: You cannot switch between streams, so choose the pathway that matches your current thinking.

Stream A

Start with Learning Outcomes

Best for educators who have clear CLOs and want to test how they distribute across assignments.

You provide:

CLO weightings: Estimate the relative importance of each CLO as percentages (e.g., CLO1: 30%, CLO2: 25%).

CLO mapping to assignments: Indicate the relative importance of each CLO assessed in each assignment.

The tool calculates:

Estimated assignment weights: Based on your CLO distribution, the tool suggests what each assignment should weigh.

Rubric criteria composition: The estimated CLO influence within each assignment's rubric, indicating how marking criteria should be distributed across CLOs.

You then evaluate:

Do these outputs match your intuition? If not, iterate by adjusting your inputs until the outputs align with what you believe the course should achieve.

Stream B

Start with Assignments

Best for educators who have strong clarity about their assignment tasks and rubric criteria.

You provide:

Assignment criteria mapped to CLOs: For each assignment, list your criteria and indicate which CLO each criterion assesses, plus a total marks value for that criterion.

Assignment weights: Input the weighting of each assignment task.

The tool calculates:

CLO weightings: Based on your assignment structure, the tool suggests how much each CLO contributes to the overall course grade.

You then evaluate:

Do these CLO weightings match your intended emphasis? If not, iterate by adjusting your assignment criteria or weights until the CLO distribution reflects your course's priorities.

Iteration is key. The tool is designed for iteration, not perfection on the first try. Each iteration deepens your understanding of how your course components interact, exactly the process we encourage in learners through constructive alignment.

◇ When to Use This Tool Use cases for verification, redevelopment, and new course design
✓

Verification

Test your existing course design. Input your current CLO weightings and assignment mappings to see if the calculated outputs match what you've already designed.

⟳

Course Redevelopment

Evaluate existing courses alongside new ideas. See how proposed changes affect the entire system before committing to revisions.

✦

New Course Design

Use the tool as a structural scaffold. Start with what you know best and let the tool guide decisions about the other components.

🔹 Solo Use

Work through the tool independently, iterating until you're satisfied with the alignment.

🔹 Collaborative Use

The most powerful application involves conversation with a Learning Designer who can ask questions that provoke deeper thinking about your course design.
