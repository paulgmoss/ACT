Stream B: Assignments → Learning Outcomes

A step-by-step guide to inputting your data into the Assignment to CLO Weightings Calculator. Follow along before (or while) using the actual tool.

Step 1 of 6

Before You Begin

Stream B is for educators who have strong clarity about their assignment tasks and rubric criteria. You start from what you know (your assignments) and the tool calculates the CLO weightings that result from your design.

Key word

Estimate. As with Stream A, the tool works with estimates. You will input your assignment structure and rubric criteria, and the tool will calculate the implied CLO weightings. These outputs are estimates based on your inputs, not precise targets.

Stream B asks you to provide one set of inputs (repeated for each assignment):

Table 1: Assignments and Rubric Criteria

For each assignment, provide the assignment name, its weighting as a percentage of the course grade, and the rubric criteria. Each criterion is mapped to one or more CLOs with a percentage allocation, plus a total marks value.

Based on these inputs, the tool calculates:

Table 2: CLO Contribution Results

The estimated overall weighting of each CLO across the course, showing how much each CLO contributes to each assignment and to the total course grade.

You then evaluate whether the calculated CLO weightings match your intended emphasis. If they do not, the tool has identified a need to iterate and reconsider your inputs.

Step 2 of 6

Table 1: Assignments and Rubric Criteria

For each assignment in your course, you provide three things: the assignment name, its weighting as a percentage of the total course grade, and the rubric criteria with their CLO mappings plus a total marks value for each criterion.

Structure

You will repeat this process for each assignment in your course. The tool provides an "Add Assignment" button to create additional assignment blocks. Each assignment block contains its own set of rubric criteria.

What to notice

In this example, Assignment 1 is worth 20% of the course grade and has 5 criteria totalling 100 marks. Each criterion is mapped to a CLO. Notice that CLO 1 appears in two criteria (Criteria 1 and 2) and CLO 4 also appears in two criteria (Criteria 4 and 5). This is perfectly normal. A CLO's influence on an assignment is the combined effect of all the criteria mapped to it.

For example, CLO 1's influence on Assignment 1 comes from Criteria 1 (20 marks) and Criteria 2 (30 marks), giving it 50 out of 100 marks, or 50% of the assignment.s rubric.

Step 3 of 6

CLO Allocations Within Criteria

Each criterion must be mapped to at least one CLO. Some criteria may assess more than one CLO. When this happens, you indicate the relative influence of each CLO on that criterion.

Criteria mapped to multiple CLOs

Look at Criteria 2 in Assignment 2 above. It is mapped to both CLO 1 (50%) and CLO 2 (50%). This means the 30 marks for that criterion are split equally between the two CLOs. The allocations for a single criterion must always total 100%.

The split does not have to be equal. If CLO 1 had 70% influence and CLO 2 had 30%, you would enter those percentages instead. The key is to indicate the relative influence of each CLO on that particular criterion.

Single-CLO criteria

When a criterion is mapped to only one CLO, the allocation is simply 100%. Most criteria will likely be mapped to a single CLO. The multi-CLO scenario is available for criteria that genuinely assess more than one outcome.

Step 4 of 6

Completing All Assignments

You repeat the process for every assignment in your course. Each assignment has its own weighting, criteria, and CLO mappings. The assignment weightings must total 100%.

Example: A three-assignment course

Assignment 1: 20% : 5 criteria (CLOs 1, 1, 3, 4, 4)
Assignment 2: 40% : 4 criteria (CLOs 1, 1+2, 3, 4)
Assignment 3: 40% : 4 criteria (CLOs 2, 2, 3, 4)

Notice how the number of criteria can differ between assignments, and CLOs can appear in different combinations across the course. The tool aggregates all of this to calculate the overall CLO weightings.

Things to check before moving on

Assignment weightings total 100%. The tool shows a "Total Assignment Weightings" bar that confirms this.

Total marks within each assignment are consistent. It does not matter whether you use 100, 50, or any other total, as long as the marks reflect the relative importance of each criterion within that assignment.

CLO allocations within each criterion total 100%. The tool displays a "Total" indicator next to each criterion's CLO allocations.

Step 5 of 6

Evaluate the Outputs

Once you have entered all your assignments, the tool calculates the CLO Contribution Results. Remember that these outputs are estimates based on your inputs. If the numbers are close to what you expected (within about 5%), the alignment is working. If there is a significant discrepancy, adjustments should be made to your inputs.

What this tells you

The Overall column shows the estimated weighting of each CLO across the entire course. In this example: CLO 1 ≈ 30%, CLO 2 ≈ 30%, CLO 3 ≈ 20%, CLO 4 ≈ 20% (rounding to the nearest 5%).

Ask yourself: do these CLO weightings match your intended emphasis? You can also evaluate them against the energy each CLO demands in your teaching sequence. If a CLO takes up significant teaching time but has a low weighting, or vice versa, that may indicate a misalignment worth investigating.

Remember: iterate

If the outputs do not match your intuition, the tool has identified a need to iterate and reconsider the inputs you provided. Go back and adjust your rubric criteria, marks allocations, or CLO mappings. The next step explains how to make these adjustments.

Step 6 of 6

How to Iterate

When the calculated CLO weightings do not match your intended emphasis, you need to adjust your inputs. The tool updates all outputs instantly as you make changes, so you can see the effect of each adjustment in real time.

The core principle

To increase a CLO's overall weighting, you need to give it more influence in one or more assignments. To decrease a CLO's weighting, you reduce its influence. Because the total must remain at 100%, increasing one CLO's weighting means borrowing from one or more of the others.

Scenario: Increasing CLO 3 from 20% to 30%

Suppose you review the output and decide that CLO 3 should have 30% influence rather than 20%. That means you need to find 10% from other CLOs. Here is one way to approach it:

Current: CLO 1: 32%, CLO 2: 30%, CLO 3: 20%, CLO 4: 18%
Target: CLO 1: 30%, CLO 2: 25%, CLO 3: 30%, CLO 4: 15%

Adjustments:
1. In Assignment 3, increase CLO 3's criteria marks
   (e.g., Criteria 3 from 25 marks to 40 marks)
2. Reduce CLO 2's criteria marks in the same assignment
   (e.g., Criteria 1 from 30 to 20 marks)
3. Observe the updated CLO weightings in real time
4. Continue adjusting until the balance feels right

You have several levers: you can adjust the total marks for individual criteria, change the CLO allocation percentages within multi-CLO criteria, add or remove criteria, or even adjust assignment weightings if the overall course structure needs to shift.

What to expect

As you adjust values, you will see the CLO Contribution Results update in real time. Iteration should continue until the estimates are about right. Remember, you are working with estimates rounded to about 5%, so precision to the decimal point is not the goal.

It is normal to go back and forth several times. Each adjustment may shift other outputs, which is part of the process of understanding how the components of your course interact.

Why this matters beyond the tool

The adjustments you make here are not just numbers in a calculator. They have real implications for how your course is run. If the iteration process leads you to change the balance of CLO weightings, the teaching sequence will need to reflect that. For example, if a CLO's weighting increases, the teaching and learning activities that prepare students for that outcome may need more time, more scaffolding, or a different position in the sequence.

The tool helps you see these relationships before you commit to changes, so that your course design decisions are informed by the alignment between what you intend students to learn, how you assess that learning, and how you structure the teaching to support it.
