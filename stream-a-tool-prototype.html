1. CLO Weightings

To begin: Click Add CLO to add the relevant number of course learning outcomes for your course. Then estimate the relative importance of each CLO as percentages totalling 100%.

Think about how much influence each outcome has across all of your assignments and their marking criteria, not just how much teaching time it receives.

Select your input mode using the toggle (top right). Numbers mode lets you type values directly. Visual mode adds sliders alongside the numbers, which is useful for a sense check: look at the relative lengths of the sliders and ask yourself whether they match your intuition. Once the sliders are in place, you can adjust the numbers directly to round off or even out the figures.

If all CLOs are equally important, set equal weightings (e.g., 25% each for four CLOs). If a CLO supports progression but is tested less in assignments, its weighting should reflect its actual influence on the assessment, not its importance in the abstract.

2. CLO Mapping

To begin: Click Add Assignment to add the relevant number of assignments in your course. Then map how each CLO distributes across those assignments.

The values represent a ratio of relative importance within each row. The absolute numbers do not matter: 1 and 2 produces the same result as 50 and 100. A value of 0 means that CLO is not assessed in that assignment.

Select your input mode using the toggle (top right). Visual mode works well when you are thinking about relative importance: is this assignment more, less, or equally important for this CLO? Numbers mode is better when you are thinking about progression of learning, for example how much of the CLO's domain is available to assess at each point (e.g., 30, 60, 100).

You can use different modes for different CLOs. For example, use sliders for CLOs where relative importance is intuitive, then switch to Numbers for a CLO that follows a progression. The values from one mode carry over when you switch.

3. Assignment Weightings

These are the estimated weightings for each assignment based on your inputs, shown as a percentage of the overall course grade. Round to the nearest 5% when reading the totals.

Ask yourself: does this distribution match what you intended? You can use this to either inform the assignment weightings of your course, or to verify whether your current weightings align with your CLO distribution.

If there is a significant discrepancy, go back to Tab 1 or Tab 2 to adjust your inputs. The outputs will update instantly.

4. Marking Guide / Rubric Composition

This shows the CLO emphasis within each assignment's rubric. Read each column as one assignment's breakdown. For example, if CLO 1 shows 40% in Assignment 1, it means the criteria mapped to CLO 1 should account for approximately 40% of that assignment's marks (whether that is one criterion or several).

The highlighted cells show where each CLO has its strongest influence. Ask yourself: does this composition make sense for how you would design the marking criteria? If a CLO appears in an assignment you did not expect, or if the emphasis feels wrong, go back to Tab 1 or Tab 2 to adjust.

Changes here have real implications: if the rubric composition shifts, the teaching sequence may need to reflect that.
