Assignments

A guide to inputting your data into the CLO to Assignment Weightings Calculator. Follow along before or while using the tool.

Step 1 of 6

Before You Begin

The purpose of this tool is to provide a scaffold for you to consider how components in your course interact and integrate with each other.

Key word

Estimate. The tool prompts you to input estimates for CLO weightings and how they map across assignments. These are not meant to be precise from the start. The tool is designed for iteration: expect to adjust your inputs multiple times as you refine your understanding.

Stream A asks you to provide two sets of inputs:

Table 1: CLO Weightings

Estimate the relative importance of each CLO in your course, expressed as percentages totalling 100%.

Table 2: CLO Mapping

Map how each CLO distributes across your assignment tasks using ratios.

Based on these inputs, the tool calculates:

Table 3: Assignment Weightings

The estimated weighting of each assignment as a percentage of the overall course grade.

Table 4: Rubric Composition

The percentage emphasis each CLO should have within each individual assignment's marking criteria.

You then evaluate whether these outputs match your intuition about the course.

Step 2 of 6

Table 1: CLO Course Weightings

This is about the relative importance of each CLO in your course. You estimate each CLO's contribution as a percentage out of 100.

Input mode

The tool offers two input modes, toggled at the top right of the page. Numbers mode lets you type values directly. Visual mode adds sliders alongside the numbers. In Visual mode, each CLO has a slider: the filled portion shows its current weighting, and a coloured line shows the space available for it to increase. As you reduce one CLO's weighting, you free up space for the others. Drag the sliders until the total reaches 100% and the distribution reflects your intended emphasis.

Visual mode is particularly useful for a sense check: once you have entered your numbers, look at the relative lengths of the sliders and ask yourself whether they match your intuition about the course. Once the sliders are in place, you may wish to adjust the numbers in the boxes directly to round off or even out the figures.

How to think about this

The amount of time spent teaching a CLO is not necessarily the true reflection of its relative importance. What matters most is how it is mapped to assignments.

Think about all of the marking criteria across every assignment. Each criterion contributes a certain percentage to the overall course grade. Each time you map a CLO to a criterion, the relative importance of that CLO increases. The more it is mapped, the more important it must be.

More accurately: estimate importance by considering how much influence each criterion has on an assignment, and then how much influence that assignment has on the total course grade.

Common scenarios

A CLO that supports progression: Sometimes CLOs are designed so they represent progression through to more complex skills. In this case, CLO 1 may be considered as important as the others, but if assignments test the other CLOs more, that is where the actual influence must be recognised. If CLO 1 only contributes 10% to the assignments, estimate it at 10%, freeing 90% for the remaining CLOs.

All CLOs equally important: This means they will be assessed equally. Indicate equal weightings (e.g., 25% each for four CLOs).

Step 3 of 6

Table 2: CLO Mapping Across Assignments

Here you map how each CLO distributes across your assignment tasks. The most important consideration is that the values represent a ratio of relative importance across the assignments each CLO is mapped to.

Choosing your input mode

Visual mode works well when you are thinking about relative importance: is this assignment more, less, or equally important for this CLO? The sliders let you see and adjust the balance directly. Visual mode also works naturally when CLOs are evenly distributed across assignments (the equivalent of dragging all sliders to the same position).

Numbers mode is better suited when you are thinking about progression of learning. For example, you may want to indicate how much of a CLO's domain of skill is available to assess at each point in the course (e.g., 30% in Assignment 1, 60% in Assignment 2, 100% in Assignment 3). Or you may want to express which specific portion of the outcome is assessed in each assignment (e.g., the first 20%, then the next 30%, then the remaining 50%). These ideas need precise values to express.

You can switch between modes at any time, and you can use different modes for different CLOs. For example, you might use the sliders for CLOs where the relative importance is intuitive to visualise, then switch to Numbers mode for a CLO where you need to express a specific progression. In the example below, CLOs 1, 2, and 3 are entered using Visual mode, but CLO 4 requires a progression approach, so we switch to Numbers mode to enter those values precisely.

Reading the sliders

Each row shows how one CLO is distributed across assignments. For CLO 1, the slider for Assignment 2 is at full length while Assignment 1 is at half, telling you at a glance that Assignment 2 carries twice the importance for this CLO. Assignment 3 is at zero, meaning CLO 1 is not assessed there. For CLO 3, all three sliders are at the same position (1:1:1), showing equal distribution across all assignments. CLO 4 is left at zero for now because it requires a progression approach, which we will enter using Numbers mode.

Why switch to numbers for CLO 4?

CLO 4 follows a progression of learning: only 20% of the domain is available to assess in Assignment 1, 50% in Assignment 2, and 100% in Assignment 3. The specific values 20, 50, 100 express a cumulative progression that sliders alone would not communicate. By switching to Numbers mode for this row, you can enter these values precisely while the other CLOs retain the values set using the sliders.

Similarly, if you wanted to express which distinct portion of a CLO is assessed in each assignment (e.g., the first 20%, then the next 30%, then the remaining 50%), those values carry meaning beyond relative size and are best entered as numbers.

Key concept

Whichever mode you use, the absolute numbers do not matter. Only the ratio between them for each CLO (each row) matters. The tool scales them proportionally.

A value of 0 means that CLO is not assessed in that assignment.

Step 4 of 6

Evaluate the Outputs

Once you have entered your estimates, the tool calculates two output tables. Remember that these outputs are themselves estimates based on your inputs. The question to ask yourself is: do these outputs broadly match your intuition? If the numbers are close to what you expected (within about 5%), that is fine. If there is a significant discrepancy, adjustments should be made to your inputs in Tables 1 and 2.

The Assignment Total row shows the estimated weighting for each assignment. In this example: Assignment 1 ≈ 23.76%, Assignment 2 ≈ 42.02%, Assignment 3 ≈ 34.22%. Rounding to the nearest 5%, we would read these as approximately 25%, 40%, and 35%.

Remember: iterate

Step 5 of 6

How to Iterate

When the outputs do not match your intuition, you need to adjust your inputs. The tool updates all outputs instantly as you make changes, so you can see the effect of each adjustment in real time.

The core principle

To increase an assignment's total weighting, you need to borrow from one or more CLOs in one or more of the other assignments. To decrease an assignment's total, you redistribute some of its CLO values to other assignments. There are three approaches: adjusting CLO weightings only (Table 1), adjusting the CLO mapping only (Table 2), or adjusting both.

Trust your intuition

There are many ways the desired result can be achieved. If something doesn't quite feel right after an iteration, do not simply accept it. Your intuition about the course structure should be trusted. Go back and try a different approach to achieve the right distribution. The tool is designed for experimentation: each combination you try deepens your understanding of how the components of your course interact.

Choose a scenario to see how iteration works in practice:

Let's say I look at Table 3 and decide Assignment 3 needs to be stronger. This is because it is supposed to be a synthesis of the whole course, but mainly focussing on CLOs 2, 3 and 4 because they are more complex and higher level. I want the weight to be closer to 40%.

So I might reduce the weighting of CLO 1 and experiment a little with how much I reduce it by. To do this, I need to ask myself some questions. What effect will it have on the course if I reduce it by 10%, and then consequently have to redistribute that 10% to one or more of the other outcomes?

Iteration 1: Reduce CLO 1 by 10%

I reduce CLO 1 from 30% to 20%, and add 5% to CLO 3 (now 25%) and 5% to CLO 4 (now 25%).

As you can see when I make that adjustment, Assignment 3 is now closer to 40%, but Assignment 2 is still weighted more. I think more adjustment is needed.

Iteration 2: Reduce CLO 1 by another 5%

So let's go back to Table 1 and reduce CLO 1 by another 5%. Again, I need to check that doing so suits the course structure. I take CLO 1 down to 15% and add the 5% to CLO 3, bringing it to 30%.

Now Assignment 3 is at roughly 41%, which is close to the 40% I wanted. Assignment 2 has come down slightly too, so the balance is more even between Assignments 2 and 3. At this point I would check whether reducing CLO 1 to 15% of the course makes sense for the teaching sequence and the overall design.

Now, let's imagine a second scenario. In this one, the weightings cannot change, so I will need to adjust Table 2. Again, looking at Table 3, I can see that I need to borrow or redistribute some of the Assignment 2 and Assignment 1 figures.

The first thing I can do is to reconsider if I should assess CLO 1 in the third assignment. If the answer is yes, then I need to ask myself how much of CLO 1 should be in Assignment 3, and also what adding it will do to the course structure.

Iteration 1: Add CLO 1 to Assignment 3 at full weight

Currently, the mapping in Table 2 uses a logic where Assignment 2 was considered twice as important as Assignment 1. If I want to add CLO 1 to Assignment 3, I could still use that logic and say that Assignment 3 is also twice as important as Assignment 1, and add the number 100 to the Assignment 3 column to reflect that.

But now, when I look at Table 3, the Assignment 3 total is more than 45%, which is probably too high.

Iteration 2: Reduce CLO 1 in Assignment 3

So what I could do is go back to Table 2 and say to myself, CLO 1 in Assignment 3 doesn't have to be twice as important as Assignment 1 but potentially it could be equally as important, and so I might change that number to 50.

When I do that, Table 3 now looks better with Assignment 3 closer to the 40% that I required.

Again, I need to check that this suits the course structure. In this case, it means that the Assignment 3 rubric now needs to contain criteria that map to CLO 1. That may not be what I desire, and so I would have to go back to Table 2 and adjust the mapping of the other outcomes if I wanted a different result.

This scenario is coming soon. It will demonstrate how adjusting both Table 1 and Table 2 together can achieve results that neither approach could reach alone.

What to expect

As you adjust values, you will see all the output tables update in real time. Iteration should continue until the estimates are about right. Remember, you are working with estimates rounded to about 5%, so precision to the decimal point is not the goal.

It is normal to go back and forth several times. Each adjustment may shift other outputs, which is part of the process of understanding how the components of your course interact.

Why this matters beyond the tool

The adjustments you make here are not just numbers in a calculator. They have real implications for how your course is run. If the iteration process leads you to change assignment weightings, the teaching sequence will need to reflect that. For example, if an assignment's weighting increases, the teaching and learning activities that prepare students for that assignment may need more time, more scaffolding, or a different position in the sequence.

The tool helps you see these relationships before you commit to changes, so that your course design decisions are informed by the alignment between what you intend students to learn, how you assess that learning, and how you structure the teaching to support it.
