Stream A: Learning Outcomes → Assignments

A step-by-step guide to inputting your data into the CLO to Assignment Weightings Calculator. Follow along before (or while) using the actual tool.

Step 1 of 6

Before You Begin

The purpose of this tool is to provide a scaffold for you to consider how components in your course interact and integrate with each other.

Key word

Estimate. The tool prompts you to input estimates for CLO weightings and how they map across assignments. These are not meant to be precise from the start. The tool is designed for iteration: expect to adjust your inputs multiple times as you refine your understanding.

Stream A asks you to provide two sets of inputs:

Table 1: CLO Weightings

Estimate the relative importance of each CLO in your course, expressed as percentages totalling 100%.

Table 2: CLO Mapping

Map how each CLO distributes across your assignment tasks using ratios.

Based on these inputs, the tool calculates:

Table 3: Assignment Weightings

The estimated weighting of each assignment as a percentage of the overall course grade.

Table 4: Rubric Composition

The percentage emphasis each CLO should have within each individual assignment's marking criteria.

You then evaluate whether these outputs match your intuition about the course.

How to think about this

The amount of time spent teaching a CLO is not necessarily the true reflection of its relative importance. What matters most is how it is mapped to assignments.

Think about all of the marking criteria across every assignment. Each criterion contributes a certain percentage to the overall course grade. Each time you map a CLO to a criterion, the relative importance of that CLO increases. The more it is mapped, the more important it must be.

More accurately: estimate importance by considering how much influence each criterion has on an assignment, and then how much influence that assignment has on the total course grade.

Common scenarios

A CLO that supports progression: Sometimes CLOs are designed so they represent progression through to more complex skills. In this case, CLO 1 may be considered as important as the others, but if assignments test the other CLOs more, that is where the actual influence must be recognised. If CLO 1 only contributes 10% to the assignments, estimate it at 10%, freeing 90% for the remaining CLOs.

Multiple CLOs in one criterion: Within that criterion, consider how important each CLO is relative to the others mapped to it, then aggregate as above.

All CLOs equally important: This means they will be assessed equally. Indicate equal weightings (e.g., 25% each for four CLOs).

Key concept

The absolute numbers do not matter. Only the ratio between them for each CLO (each row) matters. For example, if CLO 1 is doubly important in Assignment 2 compared to Assignment 1, you could enter 1 and 2, or 10 and 20, or 30 and 60. The tool scales them proportionally.

A value of 0 means that CLO is not assessed in that assignment.

Looking at the example above, read each row as describing how one CLO is distributed:

CLO 1 row: values 1, 2, 0

A1: 1
A2: 2

CLO 1 is not assessed in Assignment 3 (value is 0). Of the two assignments where it is assessed, Assignment 2 carries twice the importance. The ratio is 1:2.

Other ways to consider the ratio

The simple ratio approach shown above is just one way to think about these values. There are other approaches that may feel more intuitive depending on how you think about progression of learning in your course. The next step outlines these alternatives.

Step 4 of 6

Approaches to Mapping

There are several valid ways to think about the mapping values in Table 2. All of them are still essentially asking for a ratio. Choose whichever framing makes the most sense for how you think about each CLO's relationship to your assignments. It may be that certain CLOs are easier to consider using a particular approach, so you do not have to use the same approach throughout, but in most cases sticking with one approach will feel most natural.

Approach A: Simple Ratio of Relative Importance

If CLO 1 is doubly important in a later assignment compared to an earlier one, the ratio is 2:1. Focus purely on the relative weight of that CLO across tasks.

CLO 1 across 3 assignments →  1 : 2 : 0
(Not assessed in A3; twice as important in A2 as A1)

Approach B: How Much of the Domain Is Available

A progression-of-learning approach. Indicate how much of the CLO's domain of skill is available to assess at the time of each assignment.

A1: 20% (only basics taught so far)
A2: 60%
A3: 100% (full domain taught)
20%
60%
100%

Approach C: Which Part of the Outcome

Also a progression approach, but more precise. Indicate which portion of the outcome is assessed in each assignment (each portion assessed once).

A1: first 20% of the domain
A2: next 30%
A3: remaining 50%
20%

Approach D: Equally Weighted Skills

When the outcome is made up of equally weighted skills and each assignment tests them individually, the ratio is simply 1:1 (two assignments) or 1:1:1 (three).

CLO assessed in 3 assignments equally →  1 : 1 : 1
Step 5 of 6

Evaluate the Outputs

Once you have entered your estimates, the tool calculates two output tables. Remember that these outputs are themselves estimates based on your inputs. The question to ask yourself is: do these outputs broadly match your intuition? If the numbers are close to what you expected (within about 5%), that is fine. If there is a significant discrepancy, adjustments should be made to your inputs in Tables 1 and 2.

Step 6 of 6

How to Iterate

When the outputs do not match your intuition, you need to adjust your inputs. The tool updates all outputs instantly as you make changes, so you can see the effect of each adjustment in real time.

The core principle

To increase an assignment's total weighting, you need to borrow from one or more CLOs in one or more of the other assignments. To decrease an assignment's total, you redistribute some of its CLO values to other assignments.

Adjusting Assignment Weightings (Table 3 outputs)

If an assignment total is too low or too high, you have two levers:

1. Adjust the CLO mapping table (Table 2). Change the values for one or more CLOs in the assignment you want to adjust. Increasing a value in one assignment column means that CLO's influence shifts towards that assignment. Because the total is proportional, this reduces its relative influence in other assignments.

2. Adjust CLO weightings (Table 1). If a CLO that is heavily mapped to the assignment you want to increase is given a higher overall weighting, that assignment's total will rise.

Adjusting Rubric Composition (Table 4 outputs)

If the CLO emphasis within a particular assignment's rubric is not what you intended, the same principle applies. To increase a CLO's share within an assignment, increase its raw value in that column of Table 2 (or decrease the values of other CLOs in that same column). The tool recalculates the proportions instantly.

What to expect

As you adjust values, you will see all the output tables update in real time. Iteration should continue until the estimates are about right. Remember, you are working with estimates rounded to about 5%, so precision to the decimal point is not the goal.

It is normal to go back and forth several times. Each adjustment may shift other outputs, which is part of the process of understanding how the components of your course interact.

Why this matters beyond the tool

The adjustments you make here are not just numbers in a calculator. They have real implications for how your course is run. If the iteration process leads you to change assignment weightings, the teaching sequence will need to reflect that. For example, if an assignment's weighting increases, the teaching and learning activities that prepare students for that assignment may need more time, more scaffolding, or a different position in the sequence.

The tool helps you see these relationships before you commit to changes, so that your course design decisions are informed by the alignment between what you intend students to learn, how you assess that learning, and how you structure the teaching to support it.
